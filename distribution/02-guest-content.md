# Workstream 02 — Guest Content

## What this workstream does

A guest article on an authoritative third-party site does three things at once: it earns a contextual backlink from a trusted domain, it puts a founder's framing in front of an editorial audience that pre-trusts the publication, and it gives Google a citation pattern ("Paradigm Consulting" mentioned in a context other than their own marketing) that strengthens the brand's E-E-A-T signal.

The work is heavier per piece than podcast pitching — research, writing, editor revisions — but the asset is durable. A well-placed article on Direct Selling News or Insurance Journal will continue surfacing in search for years. One published guest article per month per founder is the right cadence: enough to compound, infrequent enough to keep the bar high.

Guest content is run by the operator (sourcing publications, pitching editors, coordinating revisions) with the founder writing the draft. Drafts are ghost-writable if the founder doesn't have time, but the founder must read and substantively edit every piece before submission. Generic ghostwritten guest content is detectable and damages credibility.

## Sourcing process

### 1. Find the right editor at each target publication

- Open the publication. Find articles published in the last 90 days that are tonally and topically similar to your pitch.
- Note the byline. If the byline is a staff writer, find the section editor (usually listed in masthead or via LinkedIn search "[Publication name] editor").
- For contributor programs (SHMR, Forbes Councils, JD Supra), the contributor onboarding form is the right door — no editor pitch needed.
- Save: publication, editor name, editor email (Apollo or LinkedIn Premium InMail backup), published cadence, typical word count.

### 2. Read three recent pieces before pitching

- Note tone, structure (do they accept first-person? do they require data citations? do they cap at 800 or 1500 words?), and whether contributed pieces are clearly labeled vs. integrated.
- A pitch that violates the publication's voice or format gets rejected even with a strong angle.

### 3. Cross-warm through podcast appearances

- If the founder has already recorded a podcast that mentions topics relevant to the publication, the pitch becomes warm: "Following on the [PODCAST] conversation where I mentioned [TOPIC], here's a piece I'd write to expand it."

## Drafting process (per article)

Each piece is roughly 4-8 hours of founder time end-to-end.

1. **Outline (45 min, founder).** Thesis, three sub-arguments, data points, opening hook, closing CTA.
2. **First draft (2-4 hours, founder or ghost).** Target word count. Write to the publication's tone, not Paradigm's. No marketing language.
3. **Self-edit pass (30 min, founder).** Cut adjectives. Cut adverbs. Cut hedges. Cut anything that sounds like an ad.
4. **Operator edit pass (30 min).** Format to publication standard. Add citations. Insert author bio + headshot links.
5. **Submission.**
6. **Editor revisions (variable).** Some publications send 1-2 revision rounds. Honor them; do not push back unless an edit changes the argument materially.

---

## Hard rules for the draft

- **No "we at Paradigm…"** language. The piece is a practitioner essay, not marketing.
- **Cite real data.** "70% of direct-sales orgs" needs a source or a credible primary observation ("across 38 compliance engagements I've run, the pattern shows…"). Vague stats damage E-E-A-T more than they help.
- **One specific story.** Every piece should include at least one short anonymized operator story to anchor the argument.
- **One link back to Paradigm.** Most publications will allow exactly one. Make it count — link to the most relevant pillar page (e.g., compliance-spine for a compliance piece, technology-systems for an AI piece), not the homepage.
- **One contextual link to the team page.** Often the bio block can house this: "[FOUNDER NAME] is COO of Paradigm Consulting and the author of the firm's compliance framework. paradigmconsulting.io/team."

## Post-publication amplification

The day the piece goes live:

1. **LinkedIn post from the founder.** Quote the single sharpest sentence. Link the article. Tag the publication.
2. **LinkedIn share to Paradigm company page.** Re-share with one-line context.
3. **Email to Paradigm list.** Short paragraph + link.
4. **Update /team page.** Add to the founder's "Recent writing" list.
5. **Add to the relevant pillar page.** If the article expands on compliance, add a "Recent writing" sidebar link to /compliance-systems. Same for culture and tech.
6. **Pitch the piece to the next round of podcasts.** A published article is the strongest warm-pitch credential a founder can carry.
7. **Track in the spreadsheet.** Date, publication, URL, DR of publishing domain, referring traffic, branded search lift.

## Cadence and quotas

- **Sourcing:** 6-8 target publications per founder, refreshed quarterly.
- **Pitching:** 1-2 pitches per founder per month. Higher volume isn't useful — editor relationships are slow and high-quality.
- **Drafting:** 1 published piece per founder per month is the target. Time-box drafting strictly; don't let any one piece consume more than 8 hours of founder time.
- **Publishing lag:** 4-12 weeks between acceptance and publication is normal.

Acceptance rate benchmarks:
- **Below 10%:** Pitch is generic or audience-misfit. Audit angles.
- **15-30%:** Working. Continue.
- **Above 40%:** Either you're under-pitching the bar (target higher-DR publications) or the angles are unusually strong.

---

## When this is working

- 1+ published piece per founder per month after the lag washes out.
- 3+ referring domains per piece (the publication + 2 secondary citations).
- Branded search for the founder's name rising in GSC after each piece.
- Editors at Tier 1 publications starting to inbound-pitch follow-up pieces.
- The articles themselves becoming the credential the founder leads with in the next round of podcast and partnership pitches.
